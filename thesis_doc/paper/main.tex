\begin{abstract}
Vision--language models are now used to answer questions about documents, charts, and normal images.
For this kind of work, the answer alone is not always enough.
If the model gives an explanation, that explanation should also be connected to the image.
This is especially important for charts and documents, where the evidence is usually visible somewhere in the input.
In the literature, contrastive training, generative training, and explanation-based training are often tested with different models, datasets, or compute budgets.
That makes it hard to know what the training objective itself is doing.
This study compares the objective under one resource-constrained pipeline rather than comparing unrelated systems.
It evaluates BLIP-2 generative fine-tuning, BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced fine-tuning, Qwen2-VL answer-only fine-tuning, Qwen2-VL rationale-generative fine-tuning, and Qwen2-VL explanation-aware fine-tuning.
The experiments use ScienceQA, A-OKVQA, ChartQA, DocVQA, and a VQAv2 subset, with capped single-seed runs and the same evaluation code wherever possible.
On ScienceQA, Qwen2-VL-2B reaches 0.775 accuracy with both rationale-generative training and the best low-answer-weight explanation-aware setting, while the fixed balanced setting reaches 0.740.
On A-OKVQA, explanation-aware training reaches 0.795 accuracy, slightly above the rationale-generative control at 0.790.
For domains without gold rationales, the completed ChartQA, DocVQA, and VQAv2 runs are answer-only fallback runs, improving their native headline metrics but not testing rationale supervision.
BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced training does not beat the BLIP-2 generative control on ScienceQA (0.480 versus 0.495), and retrieval diagnostics are used to check whether the image--answer space improves beyond frozen and random controls.
The masking analysis is reported as evidence sensitivity, not as proof of faithful rationale text.
Overall, the results support a careful view of explanation-aware alignment: rationale supervision can help under a single-GPU budget, but answer-only controls, objective balance, model scale, transfer, and evidence sensitivity must be reported separately.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Vision--language models are becoming a normal way to answer questions about images, documents, and charts.
The use case is simple: a user gives an image and a question, and the model should return an answer that is actually supported by the visual input.
For documents, the answer can be hidden in text, layout, tables, or small local regions.
For charts, the answer can depend on axis labels, bar heights, colours, or trends that are not written directly as sentences.
For science and commonsense VQA, the model often has to combine the image with background knowledge.
In all of these cases, a correct-looking answer is not enough if the reasoning is not grounded.
A model can sound confident and still use the wrong evidence.
This is why this study studies both the answer and the explanation.

The technical idea behind these systems is cross-modal alignment: the visual signal has to be connected to the language model in a useful way.
Recent work shows that this behaviour depends on many choices, including visual resolution, data mixture, connector design, instruction tuning, and the adaptation method~\cite{laurencon2024whatmatters,zhang2025mm15,deitke2025molmo}.
Open model families such as Qwen2-VL, BLIP-2, and LLaVA-OneVision make this kind of controlled study possible because they provide strong backbones that can still be adapted in an academic setting~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl,li2023blip2,li2025llavaonevision}.
At the same time, recent document and chart work shows that general visual instruction following is not enough for text-heavy and structured images~\cite{fu2025textrichsurvey,wang2024docllm,masry2024chartinstruct,masry2025chartgemma}.
The practical point is also important.
Most students and small research teams cannot fully fine-tune a large multimodal model.
Low-rank methods such as DoRA make it realistic to update only a small number of parameters while the base model stays frozen or quantised~\cite{liu2024dora}.
That is the setting used in this study.
The goal is not to chase the best leaderboard score, but to understand what happens when the alignment objective changes under a realistic budget.

\subsection{Gap Analysis}
The gap is that the objective is hard to isolate.
Many papers change the model, data, training size, and objective at the same time.
If the result improves, it is then unclear whether the objective helped or whether the model was simply larger or trained on better data.
Contrastive learning is useful for matching images and text, but it does not directly train the model to write answers or explanations.
Generative learning trains answer generation directly, but fluent text does not prove that the model used the image correctly.
Explanation-aware training is promising because it supervises the reasoning text, but it can also teach the model to write a nice explanation after the fact.
Most hallucination and faithfulness work also focuses on natural images.
Documents and charts need more attention because their evidence is more structured and easier to inspect.
This study addresses that gap by changing the alignment objective while keeping the rest of the pipeline as stable as possible.

\subsection{Research Questions}
The main question is: how should a resource-constrained vision--language model be aligned when the goal is both accurate and faithful visual reasoning?
The following research questions make that question measurable.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item RQ1: How have vision--language alignment methods evolved for grounded visual reasoning and explanation generation?
    \item RQ2: How does BLIP-2 generative fine-tuning compare with BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced fine-tuning on visual reasoning?
    \item RQ3: Does explanation-aware fine-tuning improve answer accuracy and explanation quality on datasets with gold rationales?
    \item RQ4: How does the same fine-tuning framework perform across document, chart, science, commonsense, and natural-image domains?
    \item RQ5: How sensitive are the model's answers to visual evidence under image-masking perturbations?
    \item RQ6: How does the selected explanation-aware QLoRA setup scale from Qwen2-VL-2B to Qwen2-VL-7B?
    \item RQ7: How well do fine-tuned adapters transfer across visual domains and prompt formats?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
The problem studied here is cross-modal alignment under a realistic single-GPU fine-tuning budget.
Given a pretrained vision--language model, this study tests how the objective affects answer accuracy, explanation quality, and visual evidence use.
Where possible, the backbone, data format, optimiser, and evaluation procedure are kept fixed.
Only the training signal changes inside each controlled comparison.
The BLIP-2 pair keeps the same Q-Former training setup, and the Qwen2-VL generative and explanation-aware runs use the same QLoRA setup.
This keeps the comparison feasible and avoids turning the paper into a full-model fine-tuning study.
The goal is not to get the highest possible score on one benchmark.
The goal is to see whether the objective changes the model in a way that matters for trustworthy reasoning.
A plausible explanation that is not visually supported is treated as a failure, even if the answer happens to be correct.

\subsection{Novelty of this study}
The main novelty is the controlled comparison.
Instead of comparing unrelated models or training recipes, this study changes the objective while keeping the rest of the setup as close as possible.
The paper also treats faithfulness on structured visual inputs as a main result, not only as a few examples in the discussion.
Concretely, this work makes the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item It compares baseline, BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced, Qwen2-VL generative, and Qwen2-VL explanation-aware alignment under explicit data and optimisation constraints.
    \item It evaluates answer quality, explanation quality, and evidence faithfulness across document, chart, science, commonsense, and natural-image tasks.
    \item It uses a generate-then-score protocol for multiple-choice reasoning, so the model is evaluated after producing its own reasoning trace.
    \item It tests the scale effect of QLoRA instead of assuming that only full fine-tuning or very large models are relevant.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Significance of Our Work}
The significance of this work is practical and methodological.
Practically, it studies the kind of budget that many researchers actually have.
Methodologically, it separates answer accuracy, explanation quality, and evidence sensitivity instead of treating one score as enough.
This matters most for documents and charts because the evidence should usually be traceable in the image.
The framework is also reusable because each dataset is converted into the same neutral format and each experiment is selected through configuration.
The final results are not a simple success story.
Rationale supervision helps in the science and commonsense runs, but answer-only controls are strong and the BLIP-2 contrastive arm exposes a clear negative result.

\section{Literature Review}
This review focuses on the work that is closest to the present study.
The aim is not to repeat the full history of vision--language modelling.
The review is grouped around the parts that matter here: model design, text-rich images, chart reasoning, faithfulness, datasets, metrics, and low-budget adaptation.
Older dataset and metric papers are cited where the experimental protocol depends on them.

\subsection{Recent vision--language model design}
Recent VLMs are not built around one simple alignment trick.
Their behaviour depends on the backbone, visual tokenisation, data mixture, connector, and instruction tuning.
Recent ablation work argues that many of these design choices are still not well isolated, which makes controlled comparisons useful~\cite{laurencon2024whatmatters}.
MM1.5 also shows that OCR data, synthetic captions, instruction mixtures, and specialised variants can matter as much as scale~\cite{zhang2025mm15}.
Molmo and PixMo are useful here because they emphasise open weights and open data, so the training recipe is easier to inspect~\cite{deitke2025molmo}.
LLaVA-OneVision shows that one model family can transfer across image and video settings when the data mixture is chosen carefully~\cite{li2025llavaonevision}.
The lesson for this study is straightforward: if the model, data, and budget all change together, it is hard to say what the objective contributed.

Open model families are also important for budget reasons.
Qwen2-VL is relevant because it targets dynamic resolution, documents, charts, diagrams, and other text-heavy visual inputs~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl}.
BLIP-2 is relevant because its Q-Former provides a natural place to test a contrastive image--answer auxiliary loss while keeping the large vision and language components frozen~\cite{li2023blip2}.
These papers are not used as leaderboard references.
They show the kind of backbone that makes parameter-efficient experiments possible.

\subsection{Text-rich and document-heavy images}
Documents are a hard test case because the answer often depends on small text, layout, and local context.
It is not only object recognition.
A recent survey shows how quickly text-rich image understanding has moved toward multimodal large language models for OCR-heavy and layout-heavy inputs~\cite{fu2025textrichsurvey}.
DocLLM is a good example because it treats layout as part of the generative document understanding problem~\cite{wang2024docllm}.
OmniDocBench takes the same issue toward benchmark coverage by evaluating diverse PDF parsing cases with detailed annotations~\cite{ouyang2025omnidocbench}.
This study uses DocVQA as the document task.
The focus is narrower than document modelling in general: what happens when the objective changes while the rest of the training path stays fixed?

\subsection{Charts and structured visual reasoning}
Charts fail in a different way.
The model has to read labels, understand axes and marks, compare quantities, and sometimes reason about a trend that is not written in text.
ChartInstruct and ChartGemma show that instruction tuning on chart images improves chart comprehension and reasoning~\cite{masry2024chartinstruct,masry2025chartgemma}.
ChartMimic looks at chart-to-code generation, which is useful because it checks whether the model understands the chart structure, not only one answer~\cite{yang2025chartmimic}.
ChartMuseum is relevant because it shows that current LVLMs are still far below humans on real-world chart reasoning~\cite{tang2025chartmuseum}.
MathVista, MMMU-Pro, and MMReason broaden the same problem to mathematical, expert-level, and multi-step multimodal reasoning~\cite{lu2024mathvista,yue2025mmmupro,yao2025mmreason}.
This is why the experiment design keeps ChartQA, ScienceQA, and A-OKVQA: together they cover structured charts, science reasoning, and knowledge-heavy visual reasoning.

\subsection{Faithfulness, critique, and efficient adaptation}
The main risk with explanation-aware training is simple: the model may learn to write an explanation that sounds right without actually using the visual evidence.
Recent benchmarks make this problem visible in different ways.
MMMU tests expert-level multimodal perception and reasoning across many visual formats~\cite{yue2024mmmu}.
MMStar asks whether examples are really vision-dependent or whether they can be solved from language priors~\cite{chen2024mmstar}.
HallusionBench focuses on the link between language hallucination and visual illusion in LVLM responses~\cite{guan2024hallusionbench}.
Hallucination-augmented contrastive learning treats hallucinated visual text as a representation-learning problem~\cite{jiang2024hacl}.
VISCO and Critic-V are also relevant because they evaluate critique, correction, and error detection instead of only final-answer accuracy~\cite{wu2025visco,zhang2025criticv}.
Together, these papers motivate measuring faithfulness separately from accuracy.

Efficient adaptation is the practical constraint in this study.
DoRA is cited as a recent low-rank adaptation method with the same broad motivation: improve adaptation without paying for full-model fine-tuning~\cite{liu2024dora}.
Reason-RFT is relevant because it shows the move toward post-training methods that explicitly reward visual reasoning~\cite{tan2025reasonrft}.
This study stays with supervised objective comparisons, but it keeps the same concern in mind.
The model is evaluated as a reasoning system, not only as an answer generator.

\section{Methodology}
The methodology is built around one rule: keep the pipeline fixed and change the objective.
A dataset is first converted into a shared format.
Then the selected model is loaded, QLoRA adapters are attached when needed, the selected objective is trained, and the model is evaluated with the same generate-then-score procedure.
Figure~\ref{Fig:Figure1} shows the full pipeline.
This setup is intentionally conservative.
For this study, a controlled comparison is more useful than a large leaderboard-style run.
Each experiment is selected through a configuration file that records the dataset, backbone, objective, adapter settings, metrics, and seed.
The same implementation path is used for baseline evaluation, fine-tuning, and final evaluation.
This reduces hidden differences between runs.

\subsection{Dataset}
The experiments use five public benchmarks.
DocVQA represents documents, where the answer usually depends on scanned text, layout, and local visual context~\cite{mathew2021docvqa}.
ChartQA represents chart reasoning, where the answer can depend on labels, axes, values, and comparisons~\cite{masry2022chartqa}.
ScienceQA provides multimodal science questions with answer choices and explanations, so it is useful for rationale supervision~\cite{lu2022scienceqa}.
A-OKVQA gives commonsense visual questions and rationales over natural images~\cite{schwenk2022aokvqa}.
VQAv2 is used as a general natural-image VQA control subset~\cite{goyal2017vqav2}.
Every dataset is converted into the same schema: image, question, optional answer choices, target answer, and optional rationale.
If a dataset has no gold rationale, it is treated as answer-only unless a separate rationale experiment is explicitly marked.

\subsection{Detailed Methodology}
Figure~\ref{Fig:Architecture} shows the model-level setup.
Each backbone is wrapped behind a common interface, so the training code does not need a separate path for every model.
For Qwen2-VL, the base model is loaded in 4-bit precision and adapted with rank-16 QLoRA adapters~\cite{hu2022lora,dettmers2023qlora}.
For the BLIP-2 contrastive arm, the vision encoder and OPT language model stay frozen.
Only the Q-Former-side components, the language projection, and a small answer-projection head are trained.
The baseline variant evaluates the model before task fine-tuning.
The BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced variant adds an auxiliary image--answer contrastive term.
It pulls the pooled Q-Former image representation toward the correct answer representation and away from other answers in the batch.
The generative variant trains the model to generate the answer from the image and question.
The explanation-aware variant trains the model to generate a rationale and then the answer.
So the variants differ in the supervision signal, while the rest of the run stays as close as possible inside each comparison.

For generative training, the prompt and target are concatenated into one sequence, but the loss is applied only to target tokens.
This matters because multimodal processors insert image placeholder tokens before the text.
If the prompt length is counted by hand, the supervised target span can shift.
The implementation avoids this by measuring the prompt length with the same processor that encodes the full sequence.
For the contrastive objective, the same span tags are reused to pool only answer tokens for the InfoNCE text representation.
Rationale tokens are still supervised by the generative loss, but they are not treated as the contrastive positive.
For the explanation-aware objective, the target is split into explanation and answer spans.
Equation~\ref{Eq:Loss} weights the two spans with $\alpha$.
When $\alpha=1$, only the answer span is weighted, but the target sequence can still contain the gold rationale before the answer.
A clean answer-only control is therefore reported separately when the target template contains only \texttt{Answer:}.
When $\alpha=0$, only the explanation span is supervised.
Intermediate values test whether answer and rationale supervision help each other or compete.

\label{Fig:Architecture}

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \alpha\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{answer}} + (1-\alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{explanation}}
    \label{Eq:Loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{answer}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{explanation}}$ are the masked cross-entropy losses over the answer and explanation token spans, and $\alpha$ controls their relative weight.
The experiments report two alpha modes.
In fixed-alpha mode, $\alpha$ is set by configuration and is used as an ablation knob.
In length-aware mode, the effective answer weight is derived from the supervised token counts:

\begin{equation}
    \alpha_{\text{eff}} =
    \frac{\lambda\,n_{\text{answer}}}{\lambda\,n_{\text{answer}} + n_{\text{explanation}}},
    \label{Eq:LengthAlpha}
\end{equation}
where $n_{\text{answer}}$ and $n_{\text{explanation}}$ are the answer and explanation token counts after collation, and $\lambda$ is the answer-weight multiplier.
With $\lambda=1$, this is equivalent to uniform token-level cross-entropy over the rationale-plus-answer target.
The fixed-alpha sweep is therefore an ablation, while the length-aware run is an audit control for the natural token-weighted setting.

At inference time, multiple-choice datasets are evaluated in two steps.
The model first generates a short reasoning trace from the image and question.
Then each candidate answer is scored with length-normalised conditional likelihood in the context of that generated text.
The highest-scoring answer is selected.
This avoids relying on brittle string parsing or option-label copying.
For open-ended datasets, the generated answer string is scored with the dataset's native metric.
Generated rationales are saved whenever the model produces them, and those rationales are used for text-quality and faithfulness analysis.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
The evaluation has three parts: answer quality, rationale quality, and evidence faithfulness.
Answer quality uses the native metric of each benchmark.
ScienceQA and A-OKVQA use multiple-choice accuracy.
DocVQA uses average normalised Levenshtein similarity, following the benchmark convention~\cite{mathew2021docvqa}.
ChartQA uses relaxed accuracy for numerical answers~\cite{masry2022chartqa}.
The VQAv2 subset uses VQA consensus accuracy when annotator answers are available~\cite{antol2015vqa,goyal2017vqav2}.
Rationale quality is measured with BLEU and ROUGE-L when gold rationales exist~\cite{papineni2002bleu,lin2004rouge}.
These metrics are not perfect, but they give a basic lexical and sequence-level check of the explanation.
Faithfulness is tested by checking whether the answer likelihood changes when visual evidence is masked.
The main faithfulness measure is evidence-masking consistency, defined in Equation~\ref{Eq:Drift}.
The qualitative diagnostic then compares high-drift regions with the visual evidence mentioned by the generated explanation when that inspection is possible.

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{drift}(r) = s\!\left(a \mid I\right) - s\!\left(a \mid I \setminus r\right)
    \label{Eq:Drift}
\end{equation}
where $s(a \mid I)$ is the length-normalised log-likelihood of the gold answer $a$ given image $I$, and $I \setminus r$ is the same image with region $r$ occluded.
A high positive drift means the masked region mattered for the answer.
A near-zero drift suggests that the answer may come from language priors, shortcuts, or non-visual context.
The absolute drift scale is not directly comparable across all datasets because answer length, prompt family, image resolution, and task format affect likelihood scores.
This follows the distinction between a useful explanation and a faithful explanation.
It also connects to recent hallucination and critique work, where a model may describe or rely on visual content that is not actually present~\cite{guan2024hallusionbench,jiang2024hacl,wu2025visco,zhang2025criticv}.
For that reason, faithfulness is reported beside answer accuracy, not only as post-hoc examples.

\subsection{Experimental settings}
All variants are run under the fixed-budget protocol, and any run-specific deviation is recorded in the experiment ledger.
The Qwen2-VL runs use AdamW, a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-4}$, cosine decay, three percent warm-up, weight decay of 0.01, bf16 compute, and an effective batch size of 32 through gradient accumulation.
The Qwen2-VL base model is loaded with 4-bit NormalFloat quantisation.
QLoRA adapters use rank 16 and scaling factor 32 unless a result table states otherwise~\cite{hu2022lora,dettmers2023qlora}.
The primary backbone is Qwen2-VL-2B-Instruct because it is strong enough for the target tasks while still fitting the available compute budget~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl}.
The BLIP-2 comparison uses Salesforce BLIP-2 OPT-2.7B with the vision tower and OPT language model frozen~\cite{li2023blip2}.
Only the Q-Former-side alignment components are trained.
Qwen2-VL-7B is used for the scale ablation with the same QLoRA recipe.

\subsection{Effect of Explanation-Aware Training}

Fine-tuning raises accuracy from the frozen baseline of 0.450 to a range between 0.675 and 0.775.
The best setting is $\alpha=0.10$, with 0.775 accuracy.
Accuracy drops when $\alpha$ is pushed toward the extremes: 0.675 at $\alpha=0.00$ and 0.680 at $\alpha=1.00$.
The explanation metrics show the same pattern.

The $\alpha=0.10$ run matches the generative control in accuracy and slightly improves its rationale metrics.
The $\alpha=1.00$ row should not be confused with the true answer-only control, because the model still sees a rationale-shaped target template but the rationale span is unsupervised.
The true answer-only ScienceQA control reaches 0.785 under an answer-only prompt family.
Because this sweep uses one seed with 2{,}000 training examples and 200 evaluation examples, these differences are treated as observed effects, not as final statistical proof.

\subsection{Faithfulness and Hallucination Analysis}
Table~\ref{tab:faithfulness} and Figure~\ref{Fig:Faithfulness} report the evidence-masking drift check.
Each value is the mean over 30 evaluation examples.
A larger positive value means that masking image regions hurts the gold-answer likelihood more, so the model is more sensitive to visual evidence.
The highest drift values are in DocVQA (0.376), ChartQA (0.220), and VQAv2 (0.116).
ScienceQA and A-OKVQA are much lower, mostly between 0.016 and 0.028.
Low drift on ScienceQA is plausible because many questions include strong language priors, explicit multiple-choice options, diffuse visual evidence, and coarse masked regions that may not remove the one cue the model uses.
On ScienceQA, drift does not increase in a clean way with explanation supervision.
For example, $\alpha=0.10$ gives 0.0167, $\alpha=0.50$ gives 0.0208, and answer-span-only $\alpha=1.00$ gives 0.0296 even though it does not produce a supervised rationale.
On A-OKVQA, the explanation-aware run has lower drift than the generative control (0.0227 versus 0.0283).
So the masking result supports a domain-sensitivity finding, but it does not support the broad claim that explanation-aware training alone improves faithfulness.
A manual hallucination rate is not reported because no fixed annotation protocol was run for hallucination categories.

\section{Discussion}
The results support the main idea, but not in a simple way.
Objective choice matters, but explanation supervision is not automatically better than simpler answer-only fine-tuning.
Explanation supervision can help, but it has to be balanced carefully and compared against a true answer-only control.
On ScienceQA, the best explanation-aware run is the low-answer-weight setting.
The answer-span-only setting is clearly worse for both accuracy and rationale quality.
On A-OKVQA, explanation-aware training gives a small improvement over the generative control.
However, the fixed $\alpha=0.50$ ScienceQA run is worse than the generative control.
This means the model does not improve just because explanation tokens are added.
The loss balance matters.

The BLIP-2 contrastive result is also important because it is a negative result.
The contrastive-enhanced arm was implemented so the comparison could be tested directly.
In the completed ScienceQA run, it does not beat the BLIP-2 generative control.
The retrieval diagnostic is also close to random.
This suggests that the auxiliary image--answer InfoNCE term is not enough in this small Q-Former-side setup.
For this study, the stronger supported route is generative or explanation-aware supervision, not contrastive enhancement.

The domain results show why one benchmark is not enough.
ChartQA, DocVQA, VQAv2, A-OKVQA, and ScienceQA all improve in the selected domain runs.
The interpretation is still mixed because the domains do not all test the same kind of supervision.
ScienceQA and A-OKVQA use rationale-bearing targets, while ChartQA, DocVQA, and VQAv2 use answer-only fallback training because gold rationales are not available.
That means the strong DocVQA result should be treated as an extractive document-answering result, not as proof that the model learned faithful document rationales.
Before making stronger document-reasoning claims, the setup still needs document-specific rationale supervision or a more causal OCR/layout faithfulness test.

The faithfulness results also give an important warning.
High masking drift means the model is sensitive to visual changes, but it does not automatically mean the explanation is faithful.
DocVQA and ChartQA have high drift because they depend strongly on local visual evidence, and in this run both also improve under answer-only fallback training.
ScienceQA and A-OKVQA have better answer and rationale scores, but their drift values are small and do not improve consistently under explanation-aware training.
So better rationale text is not the same thing as more faithful visual grounding.
The masking diagnostic should be kept beside BLEU and ROUGE-L, not replaced by them.

Scale gives the cleanest positive result.
With the same ScienceQA explanation-aware objective, Qwen2-VL-7B improves accuracy and rationale metrics over Qwen2-VL-2B.
This does not make the 2B experiments unnecessary, because the smaller model makes the broader sweep possible.
It does show that some weakness in the 2B runs is probably capacity-related.

The transfer matrix is also mixed.
Answer-only ScienceQA transfers strongly to A-OKVQA, while rationale-generative and explanation-aware ScienceQA transfer less well to open-ended targets.
A-OKVQA shows a similar pattern: explanation-aware training helps some targets, but answer-only training is stronger for VQAv2.
The answer-only ChartQA and DocVQA adapters transfer well to each other under the target metrics, which suggests that structured text-heavy images share some useful adaptation behaviour.
The main point is that transfer depends on both the target domain and the source objective.

\subsection{Limitations}
The main limitation is compute.
The single-GPU budget makes the study realistic, but it limits the number of seeds, model sizes, and full-dataset runs.
The second limitation is rationale availability.
ScienceQA and A-OKVQA provide gold explanations, but DocVQA, ChartQA, and VQAv2 do not provide the same kind of rationale supervision.
Any teacher-generated rationale experiment for those datasets should be reported separately from gold-rationale training.
The third limitation is the metrics.
BLEU and ROUGE-L can miss valid explanations that use different wording.
The masking metric is closer to evidence use, but it depends on the masked regions and can be noisy for dense documents and charts.
The fourth limitation is that the completed runs use capped training and evaluation subsets with one seed per full configuration.
This keeps the study feasible and auditable, but small differences should not be treated as statistically settled.
Finally, the execution logs are enough to validate the experiment outputs, but not enough for precise wall-clock or peak-memory benchmarking.
These limitations define what the paper can and cannot claim.

\subsection{Future Directions}
Future work should first repeat the strongest runs with more seeds and larger evaluation splits.
The next step is to test newer open multimodal backbones while keeping the same objective-isolation protocol.
Another important direction is better rationale annotation for chart and document datasets.
Those are exactly the domains where faithful explanations are most useful.
The faithfulness test should also become more causal, for example by editing document text, chart marks, or image regions in a controlled way.
It would also be useful to compare supervised explanation-aware training with preference-based or reinforcement-learning methods that reward visual grounding.
Finally, practical deployment work should measure latency, memory, and calibration together with accuracy and faithfulness.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper studied how the alignment objective changes vision--language reasoning under a fixed QLoRA fine-tuning budget.
The experiments compared zero-shot baselines, BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced alignment, Qwen2-VL generative alignment, and Qwen2-VL explanation-aware generative alignment across document, chart, science, commonsense, and natural-image benchmarks.
The evaluation measured answer accuracy, rationale quality, and evidence faithfulness together.
The strongest supported route in the completed runs is not BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced alignment.
It is Qwen2-VL task adaptation, with rationale supervision used carefully where gold rationales exist and answer-only fallback used where they do not.
ScienceQA, A-OKVQA, ChartQA, DocVQA, and VQAv2 improve under the selected completed runs.
The faithfulness analysis shows that better explanation text and stronger visual evidence use are related but not the same.
Explanation-aware training improves rationale text in some settings, but it does not consistently increase masking-based evidence dependence.
The Qwen2-VL-7B run shows that scale strengthens the ScienceQA explanation-aware result, while the transfer matrix shows that alignment is target-domain dependent.
Overall, the study supports a careful view of multimodal alignment: supervised rationales can help resource-constrained VLMs, but the answer-only controls, domain shift, and evidence test must be evaluated together.
