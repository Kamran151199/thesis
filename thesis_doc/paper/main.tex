\begin{abstract}
Vision--language models now answer questions about documents, charts, and natural images by aligning visual evidence with a pretrained language model.
For structured visual content such as charts and documents, the reasoning steps are often visible in the input, which makes both the alignment objective and the trustworthiness of the generated rationale important.
Prior work studies contrastive alignment, generative alignment, and rationale generation mostly under different backbones, datasets, or compute budgets, so the effect of the training signal is difficult to isolate.
It also remains unclear whether supervised explanation-aware fine-tuning improves faithfulness, or merely makes the model produce longer and more plausible text.
This study fine-tunes open-source vision--language models under four controlled variants: zero-shot baseline, BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced alignment, Qwen2-VL generative alignment, and Qwen2-VL explanation-aware generative alignment.
The evaluation spans DocVQA, ChartQA, ScienceQA, A-OKVQA, and a VQAv2 subset, and measures answer accuracy, explanation quality, and visual-evidence faithfulness under the same generate-then-score protocol.

\section{Introduction}
Vision--language models have become a practical way to answer natural-language questions about images, documents, and charts.
They are now used for document understanding, chart analytics, visual accessibility, and educational support, where a user asks a question and expects an answer that is grounded in the visual input.
Document visual question answering is especially useful for forms, invoices, receipts, and contracts because the answer is often located inside layout, text, and local structure.
Chart question answering is useful in analytics because the relevant fact may be encoded by position, length, colour, or a trend rather than by written text.
Science and commonsense visual question answering add another layer, because the model must combine visible evidence with background knowledge and multi-step reasoning.
Across these settings, a correct answer without reliable reasoning is not enough, since a model can give a plausible explanation for the wrong reason.
This makes cross-modal reasoning a trust problem as much as it is an accuracy problem.
The central motivation of this work is therefore to study how the training objective affects not only what a vision--language model answers, but also how well its generated reasoning stays tied to the visual evidence.

The core mechanism behind modern vision--language models is cross-modal alignment.
Recent work shows that the final behaviour of a vision--language model depends on the whole training recipe: visual resolution, data mixture, connector design, instruction tuning, and the way the language model is adapted~\cite{laurencon2024whatmatters,zhang2025mm15,deitke2025molmo}.
Open model families such as Qwen2-VL, Qwen2.5-VL, LLaVA-OneVision, and PaliGemma are especially relevant here because they make strong multimodal backbones available for controlled academic experiments~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl,bai2025qwen25vl,li2025llavaonevision,beyer2024paligemma}.
At the same time, text-rich and chart-focused work shows that general visual instruction following is not enough for documents, tables, charts, and scientific diagrams~\cite{fu2025textrichsurvey,wang2024docllm,masry2024chartinstruct,masry2025chartgemma}.
The important practical change is that these systems can often be adapted without retraining the whole model.
Low-rank variants such as DoRA make it possible to update a small number of trainable parameters while leaving the base model frozen or quantised~\cite{liu2024dora}.
That is the setting in which many research projects and small teams operate, so it is the setting used here.
The question is not whether a very large model can achieve the best leaderboard number, but whether a controlled and inexpensive alignment objective can make reasoning more accurate and more faithful.

\subsection{Gap Analysis}
Despite fast progress, the effect of the training objective remains difficult to read from the current literature.
Many comparisons mix together different data, architectures, model sizes, and optimisation budgets, so a gain can be caused by scale rather than by the objective.
Contrastive learning is strong for retrieval-style alignment, but it does not directly train the model to produce answer sequences or rationales.
Generative learning produces fluent answers, but fluency alone does not prove that the explanation is visually grounded.
Explanation-aware training is attractive because it supervises intermediate reasoning, yet it can also teach the model to imitate rationales that sound correct while still relying on language priors.
Most faithfulness work in vision--language modelling focuses on natural-image hallucination, while documents and charts are less studied even though their evidence is more structured and audit-friendly.
The missing piece is a fixed-budget comparison that changes the alignment objective while keeping the rest of the experiment stable.
This study addresses that missing piece across document, chart, science, commonsense, and natural-image question answering.

\subsection{Research Questions}
This work is organised around one main question: how should resource-constrained vision--language models be aligned when the goal is accurate and faithful visual reasoning?
The following research questions make the problem measurable.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item RQ1: How have vision--language alignment strategies evolved, and what principles underpin cross-modal representation learning?
    \item RQ2: How does BLIP-2 generative alignment compare to BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced alignment for visual reasoning?
    \item RQ3: To what extent does explanation-aware training improve reasoning accuracy and explanation quality across visual domains?
    \item RQ4: Which architectural and training factors most determine cross-modal reasoning performance, and how do they differ across domains?
    \item RQ5: To what extent does explanation-aware training reduce hallucination and improve the faithfulness of rationales relative to the visual evidence?
    \item RQ6: How does QLoRA compare across model scales in preserving the benefits of explanation-aware alignment?
    \item RQ7: How well does explanation-aware alignment learned on one visual domain transfer to unseen domains?
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
This study addresses the problem of learning cross-modal alignment that yields both accurate answers and faithful explanations under a realistic single-GPU fine-tuning budget.
Given a pretrained vision--language backbone and a fixed training budget, we ask how the alignment objective affects answer accuracy, explanation quality, and explanation faithfulness across document, chart, and natural-image domains.
The backbone, data representation, optimisation schedule, and evaluation procedure are held constant wherever possible.
Only the training signal changes inside each controlled comparison: the BLIP-2 RQ2 pair keeps the same Q-Former training budget, and the Qwen2-VL generative and explanation-aware runs keep the same QLoRA setup.
This keeps the comparison feasible on a modest accelerator and avoids relying on full-model fine-tuning of the language model.
The goal is not to maximise the absolute score on any one benchmark.
The goal is to isolate whether the objective changes the behaviour of the model in ways that matter for trustworthy reasoning.
This framing treats a plausible but visually unsupported rationale as a real failure mode, even when the final answer happens to be correct.

\subsection{Novelty of this study}
The novelty of this study is the controlled isolation of the alignment objective under a fixed low-resource budget.
The paper also treats faithfulness for structured visual content as a primary outcome rather than as a secondary qualitative observation.
Concretely, this work makes the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item It defines fixed-budget comparisons of baseline, BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced, Qwen2-VL generative, and Qwen2-VL explanation-aware alignment under explicit data and optimisation constraints.
    \item It evaluates explanation quality and evidence faithfulness for documents and charts in addition to natural-image benchmarks.
    \item It implements a generate-then-score evaluation protocol for multiple-choice reasoning so that the model's own generated rationale can influence the answer decision.
    \item It profiles the compute and scale trade-off of QLoRA-based alignment instead of assuming that larger or fully fine-tuned models are the only relevant setting.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Significance of Our Work}
The significance of this work is mainly methodological, but it has direct practical value.
Methodologically, it gives a cleaner answer to a common question: whether explanation supervision helps because it changes reasoning, or only because it changes the text the model emits.
Practically, it studies the compute regime in which many teams actually work, where full fine-tuning of large multimodal models is not realistic.
Documents and charts are also the domains where faithfulness is easiest to audit and most costly to ignore, since the answer should normally be traceable to visible evidence.
By measuring answer accuracy, rationale quality, and evidence sensitivity together, the study avoids treating any single metric as a complete proxy for reasoning.
The framework is also reusable, because every dataset is converted to the same neutral representation and every experiment is selected through configuration.
The resulting paper is intended to give a sober view of when explanation-aware alignment is worth the extra supervision.

\section{Literature Review}
This review is intentionally recent.
The goal is not to retell the full history of vision--language modelling, but to place this thesis beside the current 2024--2025 work that a journal reviewer will likely compare it with.
The bibliography is restricted to 2024--2025 sources, so older benchmark definitions are not repeated as references here.
The literature is grouped by technical pressure point: model construction, text-rich images, chart reasoning, faithfulness, and low-budget adaptation.

\subsection{Recent vision--language model design}
The current generation of vision--language models is less about a single alignment trick and more about the interaction between architecture, visual tokenisation, data curation, and instruction tuning.
Recent ablation work argues that many design choices in VLM construction are still under-justified, which makes controlled comparisons valuable in their own right~\cite{laurencon2024whatmatters}.
MM1.5 makes a similar point from a data-centric angle by showing that OCR data, synthetic captions, instruction mixtures, and specialised variants can matter as much as raw scale~\cite{zhang2025mm15}.
Molmo and PixMo add a useful counterpoint because they emphasise open weights and open data, making the training recipe more inspectable than in closed systems~\cite{deitke2025molmo}.
LLaVA-OneVision shows how one model family can transfer across single-image, multi-image, and video settings when the visual and instruction data are chosen carefully~\cite{li2025llavaonevision}.
For this thesis, the key lesson is pragmatic: if the backbone, data mixture, and training budget all change at once, it becomes hard to tell what the objective itself contributed.

Open model families also matter because this study is built for a realistic research budget.
Qwen2-VL and Qwen2.5-VL are relevant because they explicitly target dynamic resolution, document parsing, charts, diagrams, and other text-heavy visual inputs~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl,bai2025qwen25vl}.
PaliGemma is relevant because it is compact enough for transfer experiments while still covering a broad set of VLM tasks~\cite{beyer2024paligemma}.
These models are not cited here as leaderboard targets.
They are cited as examples of the current backbone class that makes controlled, parameter-efficient experiments possible.

\subsection{Text-rich and document-heavy images}
Documents are a useful stress test because the answer often depends on small text, two-dimensional layout, and local context rather than on object recognition alone.
A recent survey of text-rich image understanding shows how quickly the field has moved toward multimodal large language models for OCR-heavy and layout-heavy inputs~\cite{fu2025textrichsurvey}.
DocLLM is a strong example of document-specific modelling because it treats layout as a first-class signal for generative document understanding~\cite{wang2024docllm}.
OmniDocBench pushes the same problem toward benchmark coverage by evaluating diverse PDF parsing cases with detailed annotations~\cite{ouyang2025omnidocbench}.
DocVQA is used in this thesis as the document VQA task, while the cited document literature is kept to recent work on text-rich and layout-heavy image understanding.
The gap for this work is that document papers often optimise document understanding itself, while this thesis asks a narrower question: what happens when the alignment objective changes but the rest of the training path stays fixed?

\subsection{Charts and structured visual reasoning}
Charts create a different failure mode.
The model has to read text, decode marks and axes, compare quantities, and sometimes reason over a trend that is never written as a sentence.
ChartInstruct and ChartGemma show that instruction tuning directly on chart images improves chart comprehension and reasoning~\cite{masry2024chartinstruct,masry2025chartgemma}.
ChartMimic reframes chart understanding as chart-to-code generation, which is useful because it tests whether the model can reproduce the visual structure rather than only answer a question about it~\cite{yang2025chartmimic}.
ChartMuseum is especially relevant because it argues that current LVLMs still fall far below humans on visual reasoning over real-world charts~\cite{tang2025chartmuseum}.
MathVista, MMMU-Pro, and MMReason broaden the same concern beyond charts by testing mathematical, expert-level, and multi-step multimodal reasoning under harder evaluation settings~\cite{lu2024mathvista,yue2025mmmupro,yao2025mmreason}.
ChartQA, ScienceQA, and A-OKVQA are retained in the experiment design because they cover chart reasoning, science reasoning, and knowledge-heavy visual reasoning.

\subsection{Faithfulness, critique, and efficient adaptation}
The main risk in explanation-aware training is that the model may learn to sound reasonable without using the visual evidence it mentions.
Recent benchmark work makes this problem visible from several angles.
MMMU tests expert-level multimodal perception and reasoning across heterogeneous visual formats~\cite{yue2024mmmu}.
MMStar sharpens this point by asking whether benchmark items are genuinely vision-dependent rather than solvable from language priors~\cite{chen2024mmstar}.
HallusionBench targets the interaction between language hallucination and visual illusion in LVLM responses~\cite{guan2024hallusionbench}.
Hallucination-augmented contrastive learning then shows one way to treat hallucinated visual text as a representation-learning problem~\cite{jiang2024hacl}.
VISCO and Critic-V are relevant because they move beyond final-answer scoring and evaluate critique, correction, and error detection in multimodal reasoning~\cite{wu2025visco,zhang2025criticv}.
Together, these papers support the decision to measure faithfulness separately from answer accuracy.

Efficient adaptation is the practical constraint around all of this.
DoRA is cited as a newer low-rank adaptation variant that keeps the same general motivation: improving adaptation capacity without paying for full-model fine-tuning~\cite{liu2024dora}.
Reason-RFT is also relevant because it shows the current movement toward post-training methods that explicitly reward visual reasoning, even when the present thesis stays with supervised objective comparisons~\cite{tan2025reasonrft}.
This study combines the two concerns that are often separated in prior work.
It uses an efficient adaptation regime, but evaluates the trained model as a reasoning system with answer, rationale, and evidence-faithfulness outputs.

\section{Methodology}
The methodology fine-tunes a pretrained vision--language backbone under four alignment objectives while holding the dataset pipeline and optimisation budget fixed.
The workflow begins with a neutral dataset representation, constructs the model with QLoRA adapters, applies the selected objective, and evaluates the trained model with the same generate-then-score procedure.
Figure~\ref{Fig:Figure1} gives the end-to-end pipeline.
This setup is intentionally conservative because a controlled comparison is more useful here than a large leaderboard-style training run.
Each experiment is selected through a configuration file, which records the dataset, backbone, objective, adapter settings, metrics, and random seed.
The same implementation path is used for baseline evaluation, fine-tuning, and post-training evaluation.
That design reduces hidden experimental differences and makes later result updates easier to audit.

\subsection{Dataset}
The study uses five public benchmarks that cover document, chart, science, commonsense, and natural-image reasoning.
DocVQA represents the document domain and requires the model to answer questions from scanned pages using text, layout, and visual context.
ChartQA represents the chart domain and includes questions that require visual reading, numerical comparison, and logical reasoning over chart elements.
ScienceQA provides multimodal science questions with answer choices and explanations, which makes it a natural source of gold rationales.
A-OKVQA provides commonsense visual questions and rationales over natural images, which complements ScienceQA by testing knowledge-heavy visual reasoning.
VQAv2 is included as a broad natural-image VQA control subset so that the structured-domain findings are not confused with general VQA behaviour.
All examples are converted into a neutral schema containing an image, a question, an optional set of answer choices, a target answer, and an optional rationale.
When a dataset does not provide gold rationales, the example is treated as answer-only unless a separately generated rationale is added in a clearly marked experiment.

\subsection{Detailed Methodology}
Figure~\ref{Fig:Architecture} shows the model-level architecture used for the alignment experiments.
The implementation wraps each backbone behind a common interface so that training code does not depend on a model-specific forward pass.
For Qwen2-VL, the base model is loaded in 4-bit precision and adapted with rank-16 QLoRA adapters.
For the BLIP-2 contrastive arm, the vision encoder and OPT language model remain frozen while the Q-Former, language projection, and a small answer-projection head are trained.
The baseline variant evaluates the model before task fine-tuning.
The BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced variant adds an auxiliary image--answer contrastive term that pulls the pooled Q-Former image representation toward the correct answer representation and away from other answers in the batch.
The generative variant trains the model to generate the answer sequence conditioned on the image and question.
The explanation-aware generative variant trains the model to generate a rationale followed by the final answer.
The variants therefore differ in the supervision signal while sharing the same data loader, optimiser family, and evaluation path within each controlled comparison.

For generative training, the prompt and target are concatenated into one sequence, but the loss is applied only to the target tokens.
This masking detail is important because multimodal processors insert image placeholder tokens before the text, and a hand-counted prompt length can shift the supervised region by several tokens.
The implementation therefore measures the prompt length with the same processor that encodes the full sequence.
For the contrastive objective, the same span tagging is reused to pool only answer tokens for the InfoNCE text representation; rationale tokens are still supervised by the generative loss but are not treated as the contrastive positive.
For the explanation-aware objective, the target is split into an explanation span and an answer span.
Equation~\ref{Eq:Loss} weights the two spans with a coefficient $\alpha$.
When $\alpha$ is one, the objective becomes answer-only generation.
When $\alpha$ is zero, the objective supervises only the explanation span.
Intermediate values test whether answer and rationale supervision reinforce each other or compete for capacity.

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L} = \alpha\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{answer}} + (1-\alpha)\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{explanation}}
    \label{Eq:Loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{answer}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{explanation}}$ are the masked cross-entropy losses over the answer and explanation token spans, and $\alpha$ controls their relative weight.

At inference time, multiple-choice benchmarks are evaluated with a protocol that generates a reasoning trace and then scores the candidate answers.
The model first generates its own rationale or short reasoning trace from the image and question.
Each candidate answer is then scored by its length-normalised conditional likelihood in the context of that generated text.
The answer with the highest likelihood is selected.
This avoids brittle string parsing and reduces the chance that a model receives credit only because it copied an option label.
For open-ended datasets, the generated answer string is evaluated with the benchmark's native metric.
Generated rationales are stored for every example where the model produces them.
Those rationales are then used for text-quality and faithfulness analysis.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}
The evaluation has three axes: answer quality, rationale quality, and evidence faithfulness.
Answer quality uses the native scoring rule of each benchmark.
ScienceQA and A-OKVQA use multiple-choice accuracy, DocVQA uses average normalised Levenshtein similarity, ChartQA uses relaxed accuracy for numerical answers, and the VQAv2 subset uses VQA consensus accuracy when annotator answers are available.
Rationale quality is measured with BLEU and ROUGE-L against gold rationales when gold rationales are available.
These metrics are imperfect, but together they give lexical and sequence-level views of the generated explanation.
Faithfulness is measured by checking whether the rationale and answer depend on the visual evidence the model claims to use.
The first faithfulness measure is evidence-masking consistency, defined in Equation~\ref{Eq:Drift}.
The second is a qualitative evidence-inspection diagnostic that compares high-drift masked regions with the visual evidence referenced by the generated explanation when such inspection is possible.

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{drift}(r) = s\!\left(a \mid I\right) - s\!\left(a \mid I \setminus r\right)
    \label{Eq:Drift}
\end{equation}
where $s(a \mid I)$ is the length-normalised log-likelihood of the gold answer $a$ given image $I$, and $I \setminus r$ is the same image with region $r$ occluded.
A high positive drift after masking a relevant region means the answer depended on that visual evidence.
A near-zero drift suggests that the answer may be driven by language priors, shortcut cues, or non-visual context.
This faithfulness framing follows the broader distinction between a useful explanation and a faithful explanation.
It also connects to recent multimodal hallucination and critique work, where a model may describe or rely on visual content that is not present in the image~\cite{guan2024hallusionbench,jiang2024hacl,wu2025visco,zhang2025criticv}.
The final report will present these faithfulness measures beside answer accuracy rather than treating them as post-hoc qualitative examples.

\subsection{Experimental settings}
All variants are run under a fixed-budget protocol, with run-specific deviations recorded in the experiment ledger.
The Qwen2-VL fine-tuning runs use AdamW, a learning rate of $2 \times 10^{-4}$, cosine decay, three percent warm-up, weight decay of 0.01, bf16 compute, and an effective batch size of 32 through gradient accumulation.
The Qwen2-VL base model is loaded with 4-bit NormalFloat quantisation, and QLoRA adapters use rank 16 with scaling factor 32 unless the result table explicitly states otherwise.
The primary QLoRA backbone is Qwen2-VL-2B-Instruct, because it is strong enough for the target tasks while still fitting the available compute budget~\cite{wang2024qwen2vl}.
The BLIP-2 RQ2 arm uses Salesforce BLIP-2 OPT-2.7B with the vision tower and OPT language model frozen, training only the Q-Former-side alignment components.
Qwen2-VL-7B is reserved for the model-scale ablation under the same QLoRA recipe.
Each run is logged with the exact dataset split, filtered count, seed, objective, adapter configuration, and metric outputs.
Because the budget allows only a limited number of seeds, the Results section reports that limitation directly instead of presenting small differences as statistically settled.

\subsection{Effect of Explanation-Aware Training}
Table~\ref{tab:alpha_sweep} and Figure~\ref{Fig:AlphaSweep} report the explanation-aware $\alpha$-sweep on ScienceQA under the fixed QLoRA budget, where $\alpha$ is the answer-span weight in Equation~\ref{Eq:Loss}.
Fine-tuning raises multiple-choice accuracy from the zero-shot baseline of 0.460 to between 0.675 and 0.775, depending on $\alpha$.
Accuracy is highest at $\alpha=0.10$ (0.775) and decreases as $\alpha$ rises to 1.00 (0.680); it is also lowest at the two extreme settings, $\alpha=0.00$ (0.675) and $\alpha=1.00$ (0.680).
Explanation quality is single-peaked: ROUGE-L and BLEU are highest at $\alpha=0.10$ (0.706 and 0.579) and fall monotonically as $\alpha$ increases, reaching 0.000 at $\alpha=1.00$, where the answer-only objective leaves the explanation span unsupervised and the model emits no rationale.
The $\alpha=0.10$ setting, whose answer-span weight matches the implicit answer weight of uniform generative cross-entropy ($\alpha_{\text{eff}}\approx0.1$ on ScienceQA), is within run-to-run variation of the separately trained generative control (0.765 accuracy, 0.703 ROUGE-L, 0.570 BLEU).
Removing rationale supervision entirely ($\alpha=1.00$) lowers accuracy by about ten points relative to $\alpha=0.10$ and eliminates rationale quality, and moving $\alpha$ in either direction away from 0.10 improves neither accuracy nor explanation quality.
These numbers come from a single seed at 2{,}000 training and 200 evaluation examples, so the differences are reported as observed effects rather than statistically settled gaps.

\section{Discussion}
The discussion will be completed after the Results section is populated, because its main job is to interpret measured results rather than predict them.
The current manuscript therefore reserves discussion space for each major experimental theme and avoids unsupported claims.
The taxonomy discussion will explain what the literature implies about the movement from contrastive alignment to generative and instruction-tuned alignment.
The objective-comparison discussion will interpret whether the BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced arm improves visual reasoning or mainly improves representation-level matching.
The explanation-aware discussion will address whether rationale supervision improves answer correctness, rationale quality, or only the surface form of generated text.
The ablation discussion will connect observed differences to backbone choice, adapter placement, masking correctness, and prompt construction.
The faithfulness discussion will assess whether the produced rationales are visually grounded or whether they behave like post-hoc rationalisations.
The final discussion themes will cover the practical trade-offs of model scale, compute, and transfer across domains.

Several assumptions already matter before the final numbers are known.
First, the study assumes that a fixed QLoRA budget is a meaningful practical constraint rather than a weakness.
Second, it assumes that comparing objectives under the same backbone and data pipeline is more informative than chasing each benchmark's best published score.
Third, it assumes that rationale quality metrics are diagnostic but not sufficient, which is why the masking and attention diagnostics are included.
Fourth, it assumes that documents and charts deserve separate treatment from natural images because their evidence structure is different.
These assumptions shape the analysis and will be stated again when the final results are discussed.
The comparison with contemporary methods will also separate absolute published leaderboard performance from controlled within-framework effects.
That distinction is important because a specialised document or chart model may outperform the general backbone while still not answering the objective-comparison question studied here.

\subsection{Limitations}
The main limitation is compute.
The fixed single-GPU budget makes the study reproducible, but it limits the number of seeds, model scales, and full-dataset sweeps that can be run.
The second limitation is rationale availability.
ScienceQA and A-OKVQA provide gold explanations, while DocVQA, ChartQA, and VQAv2 do not provide the same kind of rationale supervision.
Any teacher-generated rationale experiment for those datasets must therefore be reported separately from gold-rationale training.
The third limitation is metric validity.
BLEU and ROUGE-L can miss valid alternative explanations, and qualitative evidence inspection is only diagnostic rather than a complete explanation of model causality.
The masking metric is more directly tied to evidence use, but it depends on the quality of the masked regions and can be noisy for dense document or chart images.
These limitations do not invalidate the study, but they define the boundary of what the final claims can support.

\subsection{Future Directions}
Future work should first repeat the strongest configurations with multiple seeds and larger evaluation splits.
The next step is to test stronger backbones and newer open multimodal models while preserving the same objective-isolation protocol.
A separate direction is to build better rationale annotations for chart and document benchmarks, because those domains are exactly where faithful explanations are most useful.
More causal faithfulness tests would also improve the analysis, especially tests that edit text, chart marks, or document regions in a semantically controlled way.
Another useful extension is to compare supervised explanation-aware learning with preference-based or reinforcement-learning methods that reward visual grounding.
Finally, deployment-oriented work should measure latency, memory, and calibration alongside accuracy and faithfulness.
Those extensions would turn the present controlled study into a broader recipe for reliable multimodal reasoning systems.

\section{Conclusion}
This paper studies how the alignment objective affects vision--language reasoning under a fixed, realistic QLoRA fine-tuning budget.
The manuscript defines controlled comparisons between zero-shot baselines, BLIP-2 contrastive-enhanced alignment, Qwen2-VL generative alignment, and Qwen2-VL explanation-aware generative alignment across document, chart, science, commonsense, and natural-image benchmarks.
It also defines an evaluation protocol that measures answer accuracy, rationale quality, and evidence faithfulness together.
